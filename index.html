Based on W.G. Clarence-Smith's 1988 article on the economics of the Indian Ocean and Red Sea slave trades in the 19th century

⚓ why are we reading this

We Know the Atlantic Story. But This One?

You have probably learned about the transatlantic slave trade in school. Ships leaving West Africa, arriving in the Americas. That story has been studied for decades and there are entire databases built around it.

The article opens with a blunt admission. More than a million people were enslaved and shipped from East African ports in the 1800s alone. And at two major academic conferences on slavery held in 1985 and 1988, barely anyone was talking about it. Almost every paper was about the Atlantic trade.

So Clarence-Smith wrote this article to sketch out what we do and do not know about this other slave trade that was happening in the Indian Ocean and the Red Sea at the same time. He is basically writing the overview that did not yet exist.

⚓ the numbers

The Scale of It Is Hard to Wrap Your Head Around

1M+
enslaved people exported from East African ports in the 1800s
Millions
more moved around within Africa itself and never crossed the ocean at all
300K
sent from the Swahili Coast to the Middle East and India
500K
crossed the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden heading north
And here is the thing. The article is honest that these are probably undercounts. Records from the importing regions are sparse. A lot of the trade happened through small boats and informal networks that left almost no paper trail at all.
⚓ who was buying and why

So Who Was Actually Buying Enslaved People Here?

This is where it gets different from the Atlantic story. It was not mainly about cotton and sugar plantations.

LABOUR

Clove farms in Zanzibar. Date groves in Oman. Construction booms around Mecca. And most surprisingly, the pearl diving industry in the Persian Gulf which employed tens of thousands of workers, many of them enslaved.

DOMESTIC

Household servants were extremely common across Egypt, Kuwait and Oman. Owning domestic slaves was a status symbol and even fairly modest families in Egypt would buy a few. Free servants would have been cheaper. But that was not really the point.

LUXURY

Concubines, eunuchs and personal attendants for wealthy households. Eunuchs were the most expensive, selling for ten times the price of an ordinary enslaved person. Istanbul was the main hub for this category.

MILITARY

Egypt briefly tried to build a huge army of enslaved soldiers under Muhammad Ali. It went very badly. He abandoned the plan after massive numbers of troops died.

A price in 1850: one person sold in Zanzibar for $5 to $10 would fetch $25 in Muscat, $40 further up the Gulf coast, and up to $100 in northwest India. The further from Africa, the higher the price.
⚓ the machinery behind it

It Was Not One System. It Was Hundreds of Overlapping Ones.

⚔️
CAPTURE
Usually a byproduct of war and empire building. The expansion of Egypt, Oman and the Merina kingdom in Madagascar all generated large numbers of captives. Violence from the Mfecane invasions spreading north from Zululand was another major source.
🚶
THE TREK TO THE COAST
Often hundreds of miles. The article points out that a lot of the death happened here, before anyone even got on a boat. Conditions in the coastal holding areas before departure were also brutal.
💰
THE FINANCING
This is one of the most interesting parts. Indian merchants from Gujarat financed roughly half the trade from Somalia down to Madagascar. Because they were British subjects they could not legally own or ship enslaved people. So they just lent the money and provided trade goods on credit instead.
⛵
THE SEA VOYAGE
Dhows carrying around 200 people to the Persian Gulf, roughly 20 days with a good wind. Average mortality on ships heading to Réunion was reported at 25 percent, which is horrifyingly high for a voyage far shorter than crossing the Atlantic.
A strange detail: Arab traders near British patrol routes would deliberately use old small ships. If the ship got captured, it was less of a financial loss. The business logic of the slave trade was cold-blooded in every direction.
⚓ the british navy shows up

Britain Tried to Shut It Down. The Results Were Complicated.

The Royal Navy patrolled the Indian Ocean for decades. But for most of that time, the honest result was that the trade got redirected. Not stopped.
1840s to 80s
The export boom years. Demand is high across the western Indian Ocean. Navy patrols push traders out of the main ports and into more remote spots. The trade keeps going, just through harder to reach routes.
1850
Brazil finally enforces its import ban. Brazil had been absorbing hundreds of thousands of enslaved East Africans. When enforcement actually bites, the supply gets redirected elsewhere in the Indian Ocean world.
1860s
The American Civil War accidentally spikes demand. US cotton supply collapses. Egypt ramps up production. Free labour runs short. Egypt temporarily increases its use of enslaved agricultural workers. Abolition in one place creates demand somewhere else.
Late 1800s
Arab traders move south. They shift operations toward Mozambique and Madagascar, areas not directly under British influence. By the end of the century almost all the slave-carrying in the Indian Ocean has passed into Arab hands.
The trade wound down when the economics changed. Not because the navy stopped it.
⚓ what the article is actually saying

What Is Clarence-Smith Really Arguing?

1
Stop using the Atlantic as your default comparison. The Indian Ocean slave trade ran on a totally different economic logic. Less plantation agriculture, more domestic and luxury demand, more maritime labour. The New World framework just does not fit here.
2
Africa itself absorbed more enslaved people than it exported. The internal trade was bigger than the export trade. But historians had been counting export numbers and treating Africa like it was only a source, not also a destination.
3
Pearl fishing is a completely overlooked sector. The Gulf pearl industry employed up to 75,000 workers by the 1900s and enslaved divers were a significant part of that. Historians have been looking at farms when they should also be looking at the sea.
4
We have been reading the wrong sources. Almost all the research relies on British anti-slavery documents. Arabic, Persian, Turkish and Gujarati records have barely been touched. The story will look different when we actually read them.
He is not just describing the trade. He is writing a to-do list for future historians and pointing out that everything we think we know is built on a very partial set of sources.
⚓ why this for our class

Why Are We Reading This in Pirates, Drug Dealers and Slave Traders?

🌊

The Indian Ocean Is the Point

Our course is about what happens in this body of water. This article maps the whole geography of enslavement within it. Not a side story. The main story.

🔍

How Do We Know Things

The FYWS literally asks this question. Clarence-Smith's answer is uncomfortable. We have been trusting British colonial records and treating them as neutral observations. They are not.

⚖️

Who Gets Studied

Two big slavery conferences in the 1980s and almost nothing on this trade. The Atlantic story had advocates pushing for it to be studied. This one did not yet. That gap is itself meaningful.

⚓

The Archaeology Angle

Dhow construction sites, trade routes, coastal holding areas. These are things maritime archaeology can actually go find. The article points toward physical evidence that has not fully been looked for yet.

What I find most interesting personally is that the article itself is doing a kind of archaeology. It is digging through what historians have written and finding all the gaps and assumptions they missed. That feels very much like what our class keeps asking us to do.

feel free to answer these questions to lead a nice class discussion!

Indian merchants financed roughly half the trade while carefully staying out of ownership. If you lend the money that makes something possible, how much responsibility do you carry for what happens?

💀

British suppression mostly just moved the trade to different places and routes. What does it mean when the main effect of trying to stop something is that it becomes harder to see rather than actually smaller?

💀
